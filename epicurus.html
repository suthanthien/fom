Epicurus#

Epicurus (341–270 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher born on the island of Samos and active primarily in Athens. He founded a philosophical school known as the Garden, remarkable for its emphasis on friendship, modest living, and philosophical therapy aimed at achieving a happy life. Unlike many philosophers of antiquity, Epicurus saw philosophy not as abstract speculation but as a practical art of living.

Although later caricatured as a hedonist devoted to excess, Epicurus in fact argued for a restrained, reflective, and humane form of pleasure centered on mental tranquility and freedom from distress.

Epicurus wrote extensively, but most of his works survive only in fragments. His ethical thought is primarily known through:

  • On Nature: a 37-book work written c. 300–270 BCE, containing extensive ethical discussion embedded in natural philosophy

  • Letter to Menoeceus (c. 300 BCE): a short but central ethical text summarizing his views on pleasure, virtue, and happiness

  • Principal Doctrines (early 3rd century BCE): 40 concise ethical maxims

  • Vatican Sayings (compiled later from Epicurean tradition): aphorisms expanding on ethical themes

Entertainers#

Note

This section applies Epicurean ethics to the role of the entertainer, defined as someone who brings delight. Epicurus’ philosophy aligns most closely with this definition. While most other ethical frameworks view entertainers primarily as storytellers, Epicurus offers a perspective that separates pure entertainment from storytelling.

Since Epicurus was neither an entertainer nor a provider of a moral framework for entertainment, many points here represent an interpretative extension of Epicurean thought. Therefore, this chapter is a supplementary discussion and does not reinforce the conclusions on professional ethics laid out in the previous chapter.

Epicurus did not explicitly write about entertainers as a profession, but his ethics can be interpreted in this context. The role of the entertainer is to bring delight to the audience and free them, at least temporarily, from pain. Human life is inevitably marked by moments of suffering, fear, and mental distress. The entertainer offers a space of relief, allowing people to step away from their burdens and, at times, to find emotional relief. In this sense, freedom from suffering is the ethical mission of the entertainer, and the means to achieve it is moderate amusement: pleasure that calms the mind and encourages temperance, which Epicurus explicitly identifies as essential to a happy life.

Epicurus observes that the greatest pains in human life arise from fear: fear of death, fear of divine punishment, fear of social ruin, and fear of the future. The entertainer’s performance should therefore help ease these fears so that they weigh less heavily on the mind. When people learn to live with uncertainty and authority without terror, their mental burden is reduced and they gain the capacity to pursue a more flourishing life. In this sense, freedom from suffering may be understood as the ethical mission of the entertainer, and the means to achieve it is moderate amusement: pleasure that calms the mind and encourages temperance.

Epicurus also observes that much human suffering arises from cravings and unmet desires. An entertainer, therefore, should not replace one craving with another, nor substitute one source of pain with a new form of desire. A performance should soothe, satisfy, and leave the audience at ease; it should not create dependency. Excessive excitement and emotional extremes are to be avoided, because they leave the audience agitated rather than fulfilled. Even if generating intense excitement brings fame and success, the Epicurean entertainer remembers that the true mission is not self-enrichment but the cultivation of lasting pleasure and temperance.

Epicurus holds that all human beings seek pleasant feelings and avoid pain. Therefore, the experience of entertainment should be pleasant for both audience and performer. It is not ethical to bring pleasure to others at the cost of one’s own well-being; neglecting oneself would contradict the very mission of delight. Moreover, distress easily passes from performer to audience: an entertainer who is anxious, exhausted, or emotionally overwhelmed cannot reliably produce joy. For this reason, Epicurus would advise entertainers not to work to the point of burnout, nor to dwell on subjects they themselves find deeply distressing or emotionally destabilizing. They should perform what genuinely brings them peace of mind.

Simplicity is another essential principle. Clear language, recognizable emotions, and shared laughter are among the most reliable and sustainable sources of delight. Constantly pursuing sensation and novelty, by contrast, leads to exhaustion for the entertainer and dependency for the audience. Novelty requires relentless escalation, which is ultimately unsustainable and disturbing. Simplicity, therefore, is not only an artistic strategy but a way of life: the entertainer remains at peace by valuing simple pleasures, the company of friends, and the cultivation of temperance.

Finally, Epicurus explicitly warns against dependence on public opinion, fame, and honor, all of which he regards as unstable and anxiety-producing. Placing one’s happiness in public acclaim inevitably leads to insecurity. Instead, the entertainer should rely on reasoned judgment and make deliberate choices about style, content, and purpose. Epicurus recommends seeking friendship rather than fame. Trust, mutual support, and shared joy bring more pleasure than recognition from strangers. Because pleasure is greatest when it is shared in stable and communal bonds, Epicurus strongly recommends friendship as a foundation for both life and art.

Epicurus’ ethical guidelines#

  • Bring pleasure that eases distress: Ensure your work allows people to experience calm and relief from fear or anxiety. Aim for content that restores mental tranquility.

  • Maintain your own well-being: Care for your health, rest, and relationships. Do not sacrifice your own peace of mind in pursuit of entertaining others.

  • Favor simple and natural delights: Offer pleasures that are clear, intelligible, and within human understanding. Avoid excessive spectacle, embellishment, or novelty that leads to agitation or dependence.

  • Do not exploit fear or superstition: Avoid inciting unnecessary dread, whether of death, the gods, or social ruin. Where fear appears, present it in ways that lessen its weight and promote understanding.

  • Remain independent of public opinion: Do not allow the pursuit of fame, recognition, or fashion to dictate your work. Choose themes and styles that reflect reasoned judgment and personal integrity.

  • Avoid stirring unnecessary desires: Do not glorify wealth, power, or status as sources of happiness. Demonstrate that contentment comes from simple pleasures, friendship, and self-sufficiency.

  • Show that happiness is attainable: Portray that peace of mind and pleasure are within reach for ordinary people. Reinforce that joy arises from understanding, moderation, and relationships rather than extraordinary circumstances.

  • Value friendship and cooperation: Cultivate relationships of trust, mutual support, and shared pleasure in your work.
