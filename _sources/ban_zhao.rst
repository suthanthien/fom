##########
 Ban Zhao
##########

Ban Zhao (c. 45-116 CE) was a Chinese historian, scholar, and teacher of
the Eastern Han dynasty. She came from a distinguished literary family
and completed parts of the *Book of Han*, one of the great official
histories of early imperial China. Ban Zhao also served as an advisor
and educator at the imperial court.

Her ethical thought is preserved mainly in shorter works. The most
important source for her views on family life is:

-  **Lessons for Women (c. 100 CE):** a short instructional text on
   conduct, discipline, mutual obligations, and harmony within the
   household.

******************
 Respectful order
******************

Ban Zhao presents **respectful order** as a household form of Confucian
harmony. Like Confucius, she believes moral life is learned through
traditional forms of conduct. Rituals, habits of deference, and
role-based duties train people to act with benevolence and
righteousness.

Respectful order takes the shape of a household in which each person's
conduct supports the life of the whole family. Speech is measured.
Assistance is given promptly. Elders are treated with reverence and
dependents are treated with care. Tension is moderated before it turns
into disorder. In this way, Ban Zhao's idea remains close to the
Confucian understanding of harmony: a fitting coordination in which
different roles and actions work together as parts of **a well-ordered
whole**.

**************
 Family order
**************

.. note::

   What follows is not a direct reconstruction of Ban Zhao’s ethics, but
   an interpretive account of the expectations implied by her writing
   for family life as a whole.

For Ban Zhao, family is the primary setting of moral life. Her advice in
*Lessons for Women* is addressed mainly to women, especially daughters
and daughters-in-law, but the household order she describes depends on
parents, elders, husbands, wives, and children each fulfilling the
duties proper to their place. For this reason, the moral logic of her
teaching extends beyond women alone and helps define the
responsibilities of the family as a whole.

**Filial piety** remains the foundation of this order, as it is for
Confucius. For Ban Zhao, filial conduct is the practice of reverent and
disciplined care through which benevolence is learned and harmony is
preserved. Her attention falls on ordinary acts such as heeding
guidance, honoring elders, and attending carefully to the needs of the
family.

Ban Zhao gives importance to **education**. She argues that boys and
girls alike should be instructed, because every family member must learn
how rites and proper conduct are maintained. This education centers on
virtuous speech, modest bearing, diligence in household work, reverent
service, and the disciplined conduct. Parents and elders teach by
guidance and modeling reverence in their own conduct.

This family order also requires ways of **managing conflict**. Ban Zhao
presents harmony between husband and wife as a union of mutual regard
shaped by distinct family roles. She asks wives to act with humility,
gentleness, and respectful speech. She also expects husbands to govern
themselves without anger, because harsh words and violence destroy
harmony and conjugal love. Marital order is preserved when spouses speak
without insult, respond to guidance calmly, show consideration in daily
interaction, and carry out their responsibilities faithfully. These
habits preserve affection by giving family members a way to handle
tension without breaking respect.

*******************************
 Ban Zhao's ethical guidelines
*******************************

-  **Learn your responsibilities and carry them out well:** Learn the
   customs and teachings that guide family life. Study the forms of
   conduct you are expected to follow. Do the daily work your family
   depends on.

-  **Be willing to learn:** Receive advice with openness. Learn from the
   example of disciplined people. Practice careful speech, patient
   listening, and steady work.

-  **Honor parents and elders through service:** Attend to their needs
   with care. Express gratitude through patient service. Show deference
   in posture, tone, and everyday attentiveness.

-  **Teach the young by example and instruction:** Model courteous
   speech in daily life. Train children in habits of respect and
   self-restraint. Make the household a place where good conduct is
   taught patiently and practiced consistently.

-  **Preserve harmony:** Yield gracefully in small matters. Show
   courtesy in the ordinary interactions of daily life. Help keep the
   household peaceful and well ordered. Keep disagreement calm and
   respectful.
