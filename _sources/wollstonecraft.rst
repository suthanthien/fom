#####################
 Mary Wollstonecraft
#####################

Mary Wollstonecraft (1759-1797) was an English philosopher, writer, and
one of the foundational figures of modern feminist thought. Writing in
the late Enlightenment, she argued that women and men share the same
rational and moral capacities and should therefore receive an education
suited to freedom and virtue. Her work joins political critique,
educational theory, and moral philosophy.

Her key ethical writings include:

-  **Thoughts on the Education of Daughters (1787):** an early work on
   education, character, and the moral formation of girls.

-  **A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792):** her central
   philosophical work on reason, virtue, equality, education, marriage,
   and social life.

***********************
 Rational independence
***********************

Wollstonecraft argues that moral life begins with respect for the
**rational independence** of persons. Women and men share the same
capacity for reason, and this capacity grounds their dignity. To treat a
person well is therefore to treat them as capable of judgment,
self-command, and principled action. In this respect, Wollstonecraft's
reasoning is very close to Kant's: because persons are rational beings,
they should be respected as ends in themselves.

For Wollstonecraft, **dignity** requires that people develop themselves
as human beings. Rational capacity is part of what makes a person fully
human, but it does not mature on its own. It must be formed through
education, discipline, useful activity, and the habit of thinking for
oneself. In this way, rational independence resembles a virtue in the
Aristotelian sense.

Rational independence is the capacity to think and judge for oneself, to
govern conduct by reason, and to resist the pull of vanity, and social
display. A rationally independent person does not live for admiration or
submit passively to custom. They act from principle, speak sincerely,
and cultivate self-command and steadiness of character. Rational
independence is thus a moral form of self-direction, and the condition
of living freely and responsibly.

Wollstonecraft's distinctive contribution is to show how social culture
can either strengthen or corrupt this moral development. She argues that
the customs of her age often trained women for charm, obedience, and
dependency. Wollstonecraft therefore advocates changes in education,
manners, and social expectations so that women and men alike can grow
into rational independence.

More concretely, Wollstonecraft argues for respect toward women who
exercise judgment instead of merely pleasing others, and for girls to be
taught history, morality, and practical reasoning instead of being
trained mainly in charm and social display.

**********************
 Family companionship
**********************

Applied to family life, rational independence means that the household
should reflect the equal dignity of rational persons. If both spouses
are capable of judgment and moral responsibility, then neither should
live as the ruler of the other. Each should meet the other as a person
with a mind, a conscience, and a claim to respect. Wollstonecraft
therefore advocates **companionship** in marriage: a life shared between
equals.

In family life, this means that spouses should make decisions together,
and share responsibility for the work of the home. Husband and wife
should consult one another about the running of the household, the use
of money, the education of children, and the moral tone of family life.

Parents who respect rational independence should also nurture it in the
next generation. Children should be taught to think, to tell the truth,
to keep regular duties, and to understand the reasons for what they are
asked to do. Parental affection should support this education and avoid
the two opposite extremes of indulgence and excessive control.

Wollstonecraft highlights **sincerity** as an essential value in family
life. It matters because affection can be corrupted by flattery,
manipulation, and the desire to please at any cost. Sincerity keeps love
joined to truth, respect, and moral seriousness. In the household, it
appears in plain speech, truthful praise, fair correction, and affection
that supports growth.

*************************************
 Wollstonecraft's ethical guidelines
*************************************

-  **Ask for reasons in family decisions:** Ask each family member to
   explain their judgment when you make plans, settle disagreements, or
   decide household rules. Listen for thoughtful answers, and help
   women, men, and children grow in honest speech and self-command.

-  **Deliberate and decide together in marriage:** Make plans, manage
   work, and face household difficulties with your spouse in mutual
   respect. Build marriage as a friendship between equals joined by
   affection, candor, and shared responsibility.

-  **Give children serious study:** Teach children truthfulness,
   discipline, courage, and independence of mind through reading,
   regular duties, and honest conversation. Praise steady effort, sound
   judgment, and sincere speech, and train them to work with purpose.

-  **Carry yourself with simplicity:** Speak with self-respect in
   conversation, present yourself with modesty in social gatherings, and
   form friendships on esteem. Measure worth by practical wisdom, and
   faithful conduct.

-  **Speak plainly when you correct or disagree:** Speak truthfully and
   with respect when a custom weakens judgment, when praise is
   undeserved, or when affection turns manipulative.

-  **Give women the education needed to support themselves:** Teach
   women to judge well, earn a livelihood when necessary, and carry
   useful responsibilities.
